The KNN Population Layer

What it is, why we built it, and what it adds on top of our existing models and Bayesian update.
Section 1

What problem were we solving?

Our ML models and Bayesian layer are good at answering "does this person's symptom pattern match condition X?" They return probabilities like anemia 68%, thyroid 41%.

But they don't answer the follow-up question a doctor would immediately ask: "and which specific labs should we check?" The Bayesian update refines condition probabilities, but still no labs. MedGemma knows medical literature — it can say "for anemia, test ferritin and CBC" — but that's a generic protocol, not specific to this patient.

The gap A user gets told "your pattern may suggest anemia." MedGemma can already append "ask for ferritin and a full blood count" from medical literature. But what if they also have kidney and prediabetes signals? Now you have three separate condition-specific lab lists, no prioritisation, and no way to resolve overlaps. The report was directionally useful, but not precise enough to be clinically actionable.
Why not just use medical literature directly? For a single clean condition, literature lookup works fine — and MedGemma already does this. The KNN layer adds three things literature cannot provide:

1. It works from symptoms, not condition labels. Literature lookup flows condition → labs. KNN flows symptoms → labs directly. For users whose pattern doesn't clearly flag a single condition, there's no label to look up.

2. It handles comorbidities. If someone's pattern matches anemia + kidney disease + prediabetes, literature gives you three flat lists merged together. KNN finds the 50 neighbours who have all those conditions co-occurring and surfaces only the labs that are disproportionately abnormal across that exact multi-condition group — ranked by how anomalous they are, not alphabetically.

3. It gives direction and relative weight. Literature says "check ferritin." KNN says "ferritin is HIGH in 38% of similar people (4.1× baseline)" — not low, not borderline, specifically elevated. That distinction changes the clinical interpretation entirely.

The KNN layer asks: "Among the real people in our database who look most like this user, which of their lab values were abnormal — and how anomalous is that, compared to everyone else?" Those ranked, directional, empirically-grounded signals become the specific test recommendations.

Section 2

The dataset we're working with

We use NHANES — a US national health survey where real adults answered detailed questionnaires and had blood draws, physical exams, and lab panels done. It's one of the most comprehensive public health datasets in the world.

The critical thing: for every person in NHANES, we have both their symptoms and demographics (what our users answer) and their actual lab results. Our users only provide the first half. The KNN layer finds the NHANES people whose first half most closely matches, and looks at their second half.

Section 3

What we tried — the journey

We started with a more classical ML approach: train unsupervised clusters on the NHANES data, assign each cluster a "fingerprint" (its typical lab pattern), then place new users into a cluster.

1

Feature selection via Random Forest

We trained RF classifiers on 883 NHANES columns to predict 7 known conditions (anemia, thyroid, kidney, etc.) plus fatigue severity (the dpq040 scale, 0–3). This ranked which of our 46 product features carry the most signal for grouping people together. Top features: fatigue severity, age, BMI, sleep hours, exercise, alcohol.

2

UMAP dimensionality reduction

We compressed the 46 features into a 12-dimensional space using UMAP — a technique that preserves "neighbourhood" structure. People who look similar in 46 dimensions end up close together in 12D. We tried euclidean, manhattan, and cosine distance metrics.

3

HDBSCAN clustering

We ran HDBSCAN on the 12D space to find natural groupings. It found 7 clusters, with 1.6% noise points. Cosine distance was the key — euclidean and manhattan only found 3–5 clusters, with one mega-cluster containing 94% of all people.

4

The insight: one cluster contains 90% of people

Clusters 3, 4, 5 had strong condition signals (5–15x lift for liver, hepatitis, kidney). But Cluster 0 — the "general population" bucket — held ~90% of all NHANES adults. Fixed cluster fingerprints don't work well for a bucket that contains everyone.

5

Pivot to pure KNN

Instead of fixed fingerprints, we switched to direct neighbour lookup. For every user, find their 50 nearest NHANES neighbours by cosine distance — then look at which labs are abnormal across those 50 people. This is personalised to each individual, not averaged across a broad cluster.

Why cosine distance? Our features are mixed ordinal + continuous (symptom severity codes, age, BMI). Cosine distance measures the pattern of symptoms (the angle between feature vectors) rather than the absolute magnitude. Two people who both have "moderate fatigue + poor sleep + high stress" will be close neighbours even if one is 30 and one is 60. Euclidean distance penalises raw numeric differences, which caused the 3-cluster collapse.

The two filters that keep results meaningful

Not every abnormal lab in the neighbourhood gets surfaced. Two gates must be passed:

Gate 1 — Minimum neighbourhood fraction A lab must be out-of-range in at least 15% of the 50 neighbours (i.e. ≥ 8 people). Liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT, bilirubin) use a lower 10% threshold because they're clinically meaningful even at lower prevalence. This prevents a single outlier neighbour from triggering a signal.
Gate 2 — Lift vs population baseline ≥ 1.5× We compare the neighbourhood rate against the general NHANES population rate for the same lab and direction. If 30% of neighbours have high LDL — but 28% of all adults have high LDL — that tells us nothing about this particular user. The signal only surfaces if the neighbourhood rate is at least 1.5× higher than the baseline. This killed the "LDL fires for everyone" false-signal problem.

Signals that pass both gates are ranked by lift (highest signal-to-noise ratio first) and the top signals go into the MedGemma prompt.

Special case: Ferritin context High ferritin is a cross-condition marker — it appears in anemia, kidney disease, liver disease, and diabetes. When it surfaces, we check whether other kidney/liver/glucose signals are also present. If yes: "likely reflects chronic disease, not iron overload." If no: "possible iron overload or subclinical inflammation — worth checking transferrin saturation alongside." This context is appended directly to the signal.

Key insight from validation The KNN layer is effectively reverse-engineering what labs are relevant from symptom similarity alone. It doesn't know the condition label — it just finds similar people and reads their labs. That it achieves 84–100% hit rates is the core validation of the approach.

What each layer actually does ML + Bayesian → "this person probably has condition X at probability Y." Condition-level signal, refined by follow-up questions.

MedGemma literature → "for condition X, the standard labs are A, B, C." Generic protocol from training data.

KNN → "in the 50 real people most similar to this user, these specific labs were disproportionately abnormal — here's the ranked list with directions and magnitudes." Patient-specific, empirical, comorbidity-aware.

For a single clean condition, MedGemma's literature knowledge and KNN produce similar recommendations. The difference compounds when multiple conditions overlap, when no single condition is clearly dominant, or when the right lab isn't the obvious one for the stated condition.

A concrete example: the ML model flags kidney disease at 72% and anemia at 61%. MedGemma from literature would recommend kidney labs (creatinine, BUN, eGFR) and anemia labs (CBC, ferritin, serum iron) — two separate lists, 7+ tests, no ranking. The KNN layer instead finds the 50 people who have this exact combined pattern and surfaces: creatinine HIGH 7.4×, hemoglobin LOW 5.2×, ferritin HIGH 4.1× (with context: "likely reflects chronic disease, not iron overload"), BUN HIGH 3.8×. Four tests, ranked, directional, with a clinical interpretation of the ferritin that literature lookup alone wouldn't produce.

That's the difference. Not "we surface labs." It's "we surface the right labs for this specific combination, in priority order, with directions."

Section 8

What this adds to the product

Before: The report named likely conditions and said "talk to your GP." The user arrived at the appointment knowing they might have anemia — but not which tests to ask for.

After: The report names likely conditions and specific tests, with population-level evidence for why those tests matter for someone with this exact symptom pattern. The Doctor Kit section can now say: "Please order ferritin, serum iron, and a full blood count — in people with my symptom profile, these are the most commonly abnormal values."

Why this matters for GP conversations GPs are busy. A patient who walks in saying "I'd like to rule out iron deficiency" will get a ferritin test. A patient who says "I'm tired" might get nothing, or a TSH (thyroid) test only. The KNN layer gives patients the vocabulary and the evidence to ask for the right tests — not just the most obvious ones.

It also handles comorbidity patterns that our single-condition ML models miss. A person flagged for both anemia and kidney disease gets a signal set that reflects the overlap — low hemoglobin + high creatinine + high ferritin (chronic disease marker, not iron overload). That co-occurrence context is something a condition-by-condition classifier cannot produce.

Section 9

The toggle — A/B comparison

The KNN layer is additive and fully optional. The existing pipeline is unchanged. Here's how to turn it on or off at each level:

Level How to toggle Effect
Per request (frontend) Send useKNN: true in the deep-analyze request body KNN runs for this user only. Response includes knnSignals block.
Server-wide (Railway) Set env var USE_KNN=false on the Railway deployment The /knn-score endpoint returns disabled: true immediately, no model loaded.
KNN off (default) Don't send useKNN or send useKNN: false No KNN call is made. Output is identical to before this feature existed.

For A/B comparison: run one cohort with useKNN: false (control) and one with useKNN: true (KNN-enhanced). Both the MedGemma output and the raw knnSignals block are logged via writeLog, so results are comparable in the log store.

TL;DR

One-paragraph summary

We have 7,437 real adults in our database who answered the same kinds of questions our users answer — and also had blood tests done. For each new user, we find the 50 most similar people (by symptom pattern, using cosine distance), look at which labs were abnormal across that group, and filter for signals that are disproportionately elevated compared to the general population (≥1.5× lift). The result is a ranked, directional list: not just "check ferritin" but "ferritin is HIGH in 38% of similar people, 4.1× more than the population baseline." This is what makes it different from MedGemma pulling standard condition-specific lab protocols from literature: it handles comorbidity combinations naturally, it tells you which direction each lab tends to go, and it works from symptom similarity — not from a condition label. For a user whose pattern suggests anemia + kidney disease simultaneously, KNN surfaces the labs relevant to that specific co-occurrence in priority order, rather than merging two flat condition-specific lists. The ML models tell you what conditions to suspect. MedGemma knows the textbook protocols. KNN tells you which labs are actually elevated in the real population of people who look like this patient.
