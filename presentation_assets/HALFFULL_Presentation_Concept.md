# HALFFULL — Presentation Concept
**Complete content proposals for 16 slides · English version**
*Tone: warm · human · empathetic — not clinical*

> ⏱ **Hard limit: 20 minutes.**
> The optional slides (13–15) are **excluded** from the 20-minute version — do not use them unless you have a longer format.
> The live demo is capped at **3 minutes max**. Slides 1–12 + 16 with demo = ~18:30. Buffer: ~1:30 for transitions and audience reaction.
> Each slide carries a target time in the Speaker Notes. Stick to them in rehearsal.

---

## PART 1 — THE STORY (Slides 1–5)
*Goal: Build empathy and recognition. The audience should ask themselves: "Do I know this feeling?"*

---

### Slide 1 — Opening / Cover

**Slide Title:** *(no classic title — a statement)*

**Core Message:**
> "You remember feeling good. Let's get back there."

**Content Proposals:**

Large text, minimalist:
> *"You remember feeling good."*

Below, smaller:
> *"Let's get back there."*

Bottom, barely visible: **HALFFULL** — app name only, no tagline clutter.

**Speaker Notes:** ⏱ *Target: 0:45*
*No introduction. No greeting. Let it sit. Pause. Then say: "That's the one thing almost every person who uses HALFFULL tells us — when we ask what they actually want. Not more energy. Not a diagnosis. Just to feel like themselves again."*

---

**Visualization:**

Picture a full-bleed dark background — deep navy or a rich, warm near-black. Not cold. Not clinical. The kind of dark that feels like quiet rather than absence. A single horizontal band of soft purple light sits roughly in the center of the slide, subtle — almost like a glow behind the text rather than a shape. The headline "You remember feeling good." appears in white, large, left-aligned, clean sans-serif — confident but not aggressive. Below it, in a muted lavender or shell tone, the subline "Let's get back there." in a slightly lighter weight. Nothing else competes for attention. No logo lockup top-right. No decorative elements. The HALFFULL wordmark sits quietly at the bottom-left in small caps. The entire slide should feel like the first sentence of a novel someone can't put down — restraint creates anticipation.

### Slide 2 — The Problem

**Slide Title:** "Exhausted. But everything is 'normal'."

**Core Message:**
> Fatigue is the most common symptom people bring to their doctor — and the least understood.

**Content Proposals:**

Left side — the facts (small, understated):
- Fatigue is the #1 reason people visit their GP
- 1 in 5 adults reports chronic exhaustion
- Average diagnostic journey: 4–6 years before an answer

Right side — the emotional truth (large, personal):
> *"My blood work came back fine. My doctor says it's probably stress. But I haven't felt like myself in two years."*

Bottom highlight:
> *"Normal on paper doesn't mean you have to feel this way."*

**Speaker Notes:** ⏱ *Target: 1:30*
*"The frustrating part isn't that medicine has no answers. The frustrating part is that millions of people go home thinking: maybe I'm imagining it. But they're not. They just haven't found the language yet — and their doctor didn't have the time to look for it with them."*

---

**Visualization:**

Split-screen composition, two distinct halves separated by a thin vertical line — perhaps just a gap, no actual rule needed. Left half: cool, structured, data-forward. White background or very light grey. Numbers sit in a column, each preceded by a small geometric accent in purple — compact, factual. Right half: warm, full-bleed with a deep teal or dark navy background. A single quote sits in the center in large, slightly lighter text — almost like a Post-it that someone actually wrote. The emotional weight is entirely on the right. The contrast between the two halves tells the story before anyone reads a word: the data says one thing; the lived experience says another. At the very bottom of the right panel, the lime-colored sentence breaks the darkness — a small light in the dark.

### Slide 3 — The Gap

**Slide Title:** "7 minutes. 2 years. Nothing in between."

**Core Message:**
> Between what you feel and what's measurable, there's a bridge no one is building.

**Content Proposals:**

Three points — as a visual timeline or map:

**The Doctor:** Has 7 minutes. Asks about the obvious. Orders standard labs. Not the villain — just a system that doesn't leave enough time.

**The Patient:** Arrives with a diffuse feeling. Doesn't know what to ask. Leaves with a lab sheet stamped "unremarkable" — and still no word for what's wrong.

**The Gap between them:**
> *"Nobody asks: How do you sleep? How does your body respond after exercise? Has something shifted in the last six months?"*

Stat callout:
> **43%** of patients leave their appointment without clear next steps *(update with cited source)*

**Speaker Notes:** ⏱ *Target: 1:30*
*"The problem isn't a bad healthcare system. The problem is a structural gap: the conversation that needs to happen simply doesn't. Not because no one wants it to — but because the tools don't exist. And that's exactly where we started."*

---

**Visualization:**

A literal gap — two floating platforms facing each other with empty space in between. Left platform labeled "Patient," right platform labeled "Doctor." Both platforms are illustrated warmly — think sketchbook style, not corporate diagram. The chasm between them is rendered in a muted purple gradient, deep and slightly hazy, not dramatic. No bridge. No arrow pointing across. Just the absence. Above the gap, the 43% statistic floats in lime text — a number that shouldn't exist, visually isolated to feel confrontational. The timeline "7 minutes / 2 years" runs underneath as a horizontal axis, making the absurdity of the ratio immediately visible without any explanation.

**Visualization:**

This slide intentionally breaks from the polished template. Use a warm, slightly textured off-white or cream background — like paper rather than a screen. A single large quote or sentence sits left-aligned, in a heavier weight, dark ink. The rest of the slide breathes: wide margins, generous whitespace. If you have a team photo that feels candid rather than staged — a working session, a whiteboard in the background, someone mid-sentence — place it on the right half, bleeding to the edge. No caption. No decorative shape. The contrast with the previous slides' graphic precision should be noticeable and intentional: this one feels human because it is. If using text only: the founding sentence runs in large type, broken across three lines, with one key phrase set in the HALFFULL lime accent color — the word or phrase that captures the exact moment of clarity.

*Goal: Show how HALFFULL solves the problem — through the user's experience, not through features.*

### Slide 6 — The Journey: Understand → Prepare → Improve

**Slide Title:** "Three steps back to yourself."

**Core Message:**

**Step 2: Prepare**

**Step 3: Improve**
*How it feels:* "For the first time in a long time, it feels like something can actually shift."
*What happens:* Armed with insight, the real work begins — and HALFFULL stays with you.

**Speaker Notes:** ⏱ *Target: 1:00*
*"We call this the energy journey internally. Not a funnel. Not an onboarding flow. An actual journey. Most of our users tell us that Step 2 is the most transformative: for the first time, they feel prepared."*

---

**Visualization:**

Three equal-weight cards arranged horizontally, connected by a thin line or a subtle upward arc — suggesting progress without being heavy-handed. Each card has its own color block at the top: purple for Understand, shell blue for Prepare, lime for Improve. The card interiors are white with generous padding. The step number is set very large and light — almost a watermark — behind the text content. Below each card, one sentence in italic describing how that step *feels* (not what it does). The line connecting the three cards curves gently upward from left to right — a visual metaphor for energy returning. The overall slide should feel like forward motion without urgency.

### Slide 7 — The Assessment

**Slide Title:** "51 questions. 10 minutes. An honest conversation with yourself."

**Core Message:**
> The assessment isn't a medical form — it's the conversation you deserved to have.

**Content Proposals:**

What's covered — written like a human, not a spec sheet:
- 🌙 **Sleep & Recovery** — Are you actually sleeping? Or just closing your eyes because you have to?
- ⚡ **Energy & Resilience** — What drains you? What gives it back?
- 🧠 **Cognition & Focus** — Brain fog, forgetfulness, the haze that won't lift
- 💪 **Physical Signals** — Muscle aches, racing heart, weight shifts, temperature sensitivity
- 😔 **Mood & Stress Response** — How much are you absorbing — and how much has stopped being "stress" and become something else?
- 🔬 **Lab Values** (optional) — If you have them: thyroid, blood count, nutrients

What makes this different:
> *"A standard questionnaire asks: Do you sleep 7 hours? HALFFULL asks: Do you wake up feeling rested?"*

**Speaker Notes:** ⏱ *Target: 1:30*
*"The critical difference: we're not asking about values — we're asking about experience. What does exhaustion feel like for you? When did it start? What's shifted recently? These questions aren't diagnostic — but they open a conversation that hasn't been happening. And that changes everything."*

---

**Visualization:**

A single app screen mock-up dominates the right two-thirds of the slide — large, clean, showing one example question in context. The question is rendered at full readability, with the answer scale visible beneath it. On the left, a vertical list of the six topic areas appears as small labeled chips or tags, each with a small icon — not icons for decoration, icons for orientation. One of the chips is highlighted (matching the question shown in the mock-up), making the connection explicit: here's the category, here's what a question in that category actually looks like. The palette is warm — no clinical white. The mock-up sits on a subtle phone or tablet frame. The key quote ("A standard questionnaire asks...") appears as a pull-quote in lime below the mock-up, centered, small but distinct.

**Speaker Notes:** ⏱ *Target: 1:30 — if running tight, skip the example areas and go straight to the "before/after" framing.*

**Visualization:**

The slide is structured around a large, partially revealed app mock-up of the completed Energy Report. The mock-up shows the top-level score or energy profile visualization clearly, and one section fully expanded — for example, the Thyroid card, showing its risk signal, a one-line explanation, and the recommended lab tests. The other sections are visible but dimmed below, building curiosity for the live demo. On the left third of the slide, a simple before/after comparison: "Before HALFFULL" in small greyed text with the phrase *"I don't know what's wrong"* — and "After HALFFULL" in darker text with *"I know where to start."* A thin purple vertical bar separates the two. The color-coded dots (red/yellow/green) from the report are echoed subtly in the slide design — turning the report's UX language into the slide's visual grammar.
